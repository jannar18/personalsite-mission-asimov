## Source 1: Mae Graphics (Desktop Folder)

13 images — sketchbook spreads, monoprints, rubbings, transfer prints, large-format texture studies.

### What These Are About

**Texture, materiality, process.** Every piece is about surface quality — how materials hold marks, how processes leave traces. The color in these (violet/blue ink) is incidental to the medium, not a palette choice.

### Patterns Observed

- **Monoprint / rubbing quality** — surfaces that feel archaeological, weathered, layered over time
- **Stipple and grain over smooth gradients** — the texture is granular, particle-based, never airbrushed
- **The hand is always visible** — pencil pressure, ink absorption, paper grain are part of the image
- **Field compositions** — many function as immersive texture fields you enter, not directed compositions with focal points
- **Architectural observation** — pencil studies of brick walls, doors, thresholds. Studying built surfaces and material weathering.
- **Scale ambiguity** — works could read as microscopic or massive. Deliberate and sophisticated.
- **Void and presence** — negative space is active, not empty

### Relevance to Site

These textures are the **material layer** of the site — the grain, noise, and surface quality that makes it feel physical rather than flat-digital. The monoprint/rubbing quality is achievable with generative noise functions and shader techniques. The archaeological, weathered surface feel should inform background textures and decorative elements.

1. **Material consciousness** — the surface always matters
2. **Restraint** — nothing is overworked
3. **Atmospheric quality** — things bleed, fade, breathe at the edges
4. **The hand is present** — you feel the making process
5. **Generous white space** — letting things exist

**Warm, earthen, atmospheric.** NOT cold minimalism.

## Source 3: Pinterest Board — "Architecture School References + Graphics / Graphics"

98 pins downloaded from https://www.pinterest.com/jmroberts18/architecture-school-references-+-graphics/graphics/

This board is a curated collection of architectural representation — how buildings, landscapes, and ideas are drawn, mapped, and communicated. It's not a mood board for aesthetics in the abstract; it's a library of **how to see and show the world architecturally**.

### Dominant Themes

#### 1. Collage as Primary Method

The single strongest thread. Photo fragments embedded within drawings, layered media, transparent boundaries between photography and line work. "Walls inside walls inside walls" — the literal caption on one piece, and the philosophy of the whole board.

- Photo-collage sections where photography and drawing coexist without hierarchy
- Panoramic site sections stitched from photo fragments + drawn extensions
- Architectural fragments (stone walls, doors, bell towers) collaged onto drawn frameworks with proportional analysis marks
- The collage isn't decorative — it's analytical. Photo = observed reality. Drawing = interpreted/projected reality. The layering IS the thinking.

#### 2. Topographic / Cartographic Obsession

Contour lines, river systems, terrain mapping. The land itself as subject.

- River corridor ecological maps (gorgeous blue-gray waterway through topographic contours)
- Watercolor site plans where landscape is painted and buildings are drawn (muted rose/sage palette)
- Landscape masterplans with hand-painted tree canopies as individual characters
- Topographic plans where hatched landscape texture has more visual weight than the buildings

#### 3. Stipple as Texture Language

Thousands of tiny marks creating mass, atmosphere, and depth. This is THE drawing technique that runs through the board.

- Aerial site plans rendered entirely in stipple — buildings are crisp geometry, landscape is stippled mass
- Elevation drawings where a house sits within stippled vegetation that dissolves at the edges
- Tree line elevations — rows of stippled forest masses at various densities
- ETH Zürich-style infrastructure sections (Sarah Greuter) with obsessive stipple fill for soil, roots, stone

#### 4. Landscape Dominates Architecture

Buildings are nestled, embedded, almost secondary to their landscape context. Architecture doesn't announce itself — it sits within.

- Houses barely visible within stippled hillside vegetation
- Sections where tree canopy is rendered with more care than the building
- Site plans where green space and water systems have more visual energy than built form
- The "entrance" composition: photograph of a garden path embedded in delicate botanical line drawings — landscape is the subject, architecture is the frame

#### 5. Drawing + Photography Layering

Not collage exactly, but the co-existence of drawn and photographic reality in a single frame.

- Analytical overlays: proportional circles and measurement lines drawn directly over photographs of Mediterranean buildings
- Nordic landscape sketches with handwritten notes in Spanish — personal observation + analytical notation
- Sound visualization as landscape: particle-based waveform sits above topographic contour drawing ("Think what does the sound is trying to tell you")
- Building section drawn in pencil where one room glows with warm photographic light — everything else is line, one space is REAL

#### 6. The Hand-Drawn Tree as Character

Trees are never stock or generic. Each one is drawn with specificity — species, age, canopy shape, root system.

- Background tree line elevation studies (stippled, varied densities)
- Infrastructure sections showing root systems penetrating below grade
- Site plans with hand-painted tree canopies that feel like portraits
- Trees as the primary visual subject, with buildings secondary

#### 7. Warm Earth Tones When Color Appears

When the board leaves monochrome (most is black/white/gray), the colors are:

- **Terracotta / warm stone** — Mediterranean building fragments
- **Coral / red-brown** — handwritten annotations, botanical sketches, accent marks
- **Muted rose / dusty pink** — building masses in watercolor site plans
- **Sage / olive green** — painted landscape and tree canopy
- **Slate blue-gray** — water systems, river corridors

#### 8. European Architectural Drawing Tradition

Strong Scandinavian, Mediterranean, and Swiss (ETH Zürich) influence. This is NOT American rendering culture — no glossy Photoshop, no entourage people, no sky replacements.

- Danish text on several pieces (Aalborg, Hovedopgave references)
- Spanish handwritten notes on Nordic landscape studies
- ETH Zürich credited infrastructure drawings
- Mediterranean proportional analysis studies

#### 9. Textile / Fiber Art Crossover

A tree photograph printed on translucent fabric with embroidered/woven roots extending below. Threads as root systems, weaving as ground plane. Material crossing between architecture and craft.

#### 10. Data / Sound as Landscape

A stunning composition: particle-based sound waveform (thousands of dots forming a frequency visualization) above topographic contour lines. Sonic landscape mapped onto physical landscape. Non-visual data made spatial.

### What the Board Reveals About Taste

This board isn't about "pretty buildings." It's about **how to represent the relationship between built form and ground**. The consistent interests are:

1. **Process over product** — showing the thinking, not just the result
2. **Analysis over illustration** — understanding through drawing, not beautifying
3. **Landscape as co-author** — the ground has as much to say as the building
4. **Material honesty** — the medium matters (pen weight, paper texture, collage edges)
5. **European rigor** — Swiss/Scandinavian precision, Mediterranean warmth
6. **Quiet virtuosity** — incredible skill deployed with restraint

## Updated Synthesis: Design DNA (All Three Sources)

### Core Visual Language

| Quality | How It Manifests |
|---------|-----------------|
| **Collage / layering** | Photo fragments within drawn contexts. Multiple realities coexisting. Not Photoshop compositing — more like a studio desk with overlapping work. |
| **Stipple / particle texture** | Thousands of tiny marks creating atmosphere. The fundamental texture unit is a point, not a stroke. |
| **Warm earth palette** | Terracotta, ochre, amber, cream, sage, slate. Warm off-white backgrounds. Coral/red as accent. |
| **Edge dissolution** | Things bleed, fade, dissolve at boundaries. Watercolor washes. Stipple that thins at edges. No hard crops. |
| **Landscape consciousness** | The ground matters. Topography, vegetation, water systems. Nature as co-author, not backdrop. |
| **Hand quality** | The maker's presence is always felt. Process marks, paper grain, pen pressure. |
| **Generous whitespace** | Compositions breathe. Negative space is active and intentional. |
| **Quiet typography** | Serif, light weight, lowercase. Handwritten annotations. Never loud. |

### Texture Vocabulary (for Generative Pipeline)

2. **Paper grain** — subtle fiber texture underlying everything
3. **Watercolor wash** — gradient with organic, uneven edges. Not CSS linear-gradient.
4. **Contour/topographic lines** — flowing, organic, map-like
5. **Collage edges** — slightly rough, not pixel-perfect. Torn paper feel.
6. **Monoprint / rubbing** — surface transfer texture, archaeological quality

### Typography Direction

- Serif, light weight, lowercase
- Handwritten annotations as a secondary voice (not Comic Sans — think architect's lettering)
- Quiet, refined, European
- Generous letter-spacing and line-height

### Layout Direction

- Generous margins and whitespace — things breathe
- Collage-like composition — elements don't snap to rigid grids, they overlap and layer
- The "studio desk" metaphor from the PRD maps perfectly to this
- Architecture portfolio pages with intentional sequencing (not image grids)
- Landscape orientation for drawings that need to breathe horizontally

## Source 4: Asimov Collective Project Sites (Experience & Structure Analysis)

> **Note:** These references are about experience, UX structure, storytelling, branding, and what makes them feel premium — NOT color. Color palette is already established from Julianna's own work.

Sites analyzed: Shinkei Systems, Monumental Labs, Extropic. (Valar Atomics pending.)

### The Asimov Collective Formula

Every project page follows the same structural DNA with remarkable consistency:

**1. Navigation = Confidence**
- Fixed header with just two links: "Library" and "Projects"
- Breadcrumb ("Back to home") for escape
- Sequential project navigation (prev/next) — portfolio is a browsable collection, not isolated pages
- Minimal navigation = "we trust our content to hold you"

**2. Content Pacing = Slow Reveal**
- Hero → title + description → service tags → image galleries → CTA → next project
- Progressive disclosure — metadata first, then rich media builds narrative momentum
- Multiple image galleries in three formats: **wide (landscape), square, tall (portrait)**
- Galleries break up text sections — the rhythm is: read → look → read → look

**3. Imagery = Full Immersion**
- High-resolution assets (4000px wide), WebP with blur placeholders for progressive loading
- Three responsive image formats serve different aspect ratios per device
- Embedded interactive "playgrounds" (asimov-playground.vercel.app) — dynamic, explorable content beyond static images
- Video autoplay where appropriate
- Images DOMINATE — text is brief, imagery does the storytelling

**4. Typography = Invisible System**
- Custom fonts (7 distinct font files loaded per page) — not system fonts, not Google Fonts
- Sans-serif dominant, clean and contemporary
- Strong hierarchy: large headlines for impact, smaller body copy
- Generous line-height — text breathes

**5. Copy = Precise and Confident**
- Brief, substantive, no hyperbole
- "Artisanal quality at industrial scale" (Shinkei) — concise positioning
- "Bridges workshop traditions of fine arts with cutting-edge technology" (Monumental) — craft meets innovation
- Tags define scope: "Brand Design, Strategy, Web Design + Development, Copywriting, Product Design, Identity Design"
- The copy SERVES the imagery, not the other way around

**6. Interaction = Subtle Physics**
- Lenis scroll library with lerp: 0.8 — smooth, physics-based scrolling
- No jarring animations — everything eases
- ResizeObserver for responsive behavior
- Scroll-linked reveals (progressive disclosure as you scroll)
- The interaction is FELT, not seen

**7. Whitespace = Confidence**
- Generous vertical rhythm between sections
- Fixed nav doesn't intrude
- Image galleries have breathing room
- Absence of clutter communicates: "we don't need to fill space to justify our presence"

**8. Premium Quality Signals**
- Custom font infrastructure
- Sophisticated image optimization (blur placeholders, responsive formats)
- Smooth scroll physics
- Embedded interactive playgrounds (not just screenshots — live explorable content)
- Detailed view tracking / analytics
- Clean, purposeful navigation — nothing extraneous

### What to Take from Asimov for Mission Asimov

| Asimov Pattern | How It Translates |
|----------------|-------------------|
| Single-column vertical scroll | Primary site structure — one column, intentional pacing |
| Three image formats (wide/square/tall) | Portfolio pages use mixed aspect ratios, not uniform grids |
| Progressive disclosure | Content reveals as you scroll — don't show everything at once |
| Sequential project navigation | Architecture portfolio as a browsable sequence, not a grid |
| Embedded interactive playgrounds | Room for interactive experiments within project pages |
| Smooth scroll physics (Lenis, lerp) | The site should FEEL physical — eased, weighted movement |
| Copy serves imagery | Brief, precise text. Let the work speak. |
| Custom typography system | Invest in typeface selection early — this is non-negotiable for the quality bar |

## Source 5: Porter Robinson / Nurture

### porterrobinson.com

The landing is intentionally **minimal and mysterious** — pink background with "Porter Robinson SMILE! :D." The real experience loads asynchronously via JavaScript (WebGL/Three.js world). The site IS a narrative device, not a container.

Key qualities:
- **Anticipation through absence** — nothing is immediately visible, the experience unfolds
- **The site as a space you enter** — not a page you read (confirmed from earlier research)
- **Performative identity** — "SMILE! :D" is an instruction, not an invitation
- Custom fonts (NBArchitekt, PPPangramsans) for artistic control
- Sophisticated tracking underneath a deliberately opaque navigation
